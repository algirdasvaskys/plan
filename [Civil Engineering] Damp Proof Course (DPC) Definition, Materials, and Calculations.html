Damp Proof Course (DPC): Definition, Materials, and Calculations

What is DPC?

DPC stands for Damp Proof Course. It is a horizontal layer of waterproof material in the walls of a building near the ground level, designed to prevent moisture from rising through the structure by capillary action - a phenomenon known as rising damp.

Importance of DPC

DPC is crucial in construction for several reasons:

  • Prevents rising damp, which can lead to structural damage
  • Protects interior finishes from water damage
  • Improves indoor air quality by reducing moisture and mold growth
  • Increases the longevity of the building

Materials Used in DPC

Various materials can be used for DPC, depending on the specific requirements of the building and local conditions. Common materials include:

Material Description
Bitumen-based felts Flexible sheets impregnated with bitumen
Polyethylene sheets Thin, durable plastic sheets
Slate Natural stone material, often used in older buildings
Engineering bricks Dense clay bricks with low water absorption
Cement mortar with waterproofing compounds Mortar mixed with additives to enhance water resistance

DPC Calculation

Calculating the requirements for DPC involves several factors:

1. DPC Height Calculation

The height of DPC from ground level is typically calculated as:

DPC Height = 150mm above highest ground level

This ensures that the DPC is above the splash zone of rain hitting the ground.

2. DPC Thickness Calculation

The thickness of DPC depends on the material used and the wall thickness. A general rule is:

DPC Thickness = Wall Thickness + 25mm (on each side)

This allows for proper overlap and sealing at the edges.

4. DPC Overlap Calculation

At joints and corners, DPC should overlap. The standard overlap is:

DPC Overlap = Minimum 100mm at joints

This ensures continuity of the damp proofing layer.

Conclusion

Proper installation of DPC is crucial for the longevity and health of a building.
