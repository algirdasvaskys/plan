Selected Problems & Solutions on Wattmeter

Problem 1: Basic Wattmeter Reading

A wattmeter connected to a circuit shows a reading of 500 W. What does this indicate about the power consumption of the circuit?

Solution: The wattmeter reading of 500 W directly indicates that the circuit is consuming 500 watts of electrical power. This means that energy is being used at a rate of 500 joules per second.

Problem 2: Calculating Current from Wattmeter Reading

A wattmeter connected to a 120 V AC circuit reads 600 W. What is the current flowing through the circuit?

Solution: We can use the power formula to calculate the current:

P = V × I

Where P is power in watts, V is voltage in volts, and I is current in amperes.

Rearranging the formula: I = P / V

I = 600 W / 120 V = 5 A

Therefore, the current flowing through the circuit is 5 amperes.

Problem 3: Power Factor Calculation

A wattmeter reads 1000 W in an AC circuit. The voltmeter reads 200 V and the ammeter reads 6 A. Calculate the power factor of the circuit.

Solution: The power factor (PF) is the ratio of real power to apparent power. We can calculate it using the formula:

PF = P / (V × I)

Where P is the real power (wattmeter reading), V is voltage, and I is current.

PF = 1000 W / (200 V × 6 A) = 1000 / 1200 = 0.833

The power factor of the circuit is 0.833 or 83.3%.

Problem 4: Three-Phase Power Measurement

A three-phase balanced load is connected to a 400 V (line-to-line) supply. The wattmeter reading is 10 kW. What is the power factor of the load?

Solution: For a balanced three-phase system, we can use the formula:

P = √3 × VL × IL × PF

Where P is power, VL is line voltage, IL is line current, and PF is power factor.

We don't know the current, so we need to use the relationship between line and phase voltages:

VL = √3 × VP

VP = 400 / √3 ≈ 230.94 V

Now we can use: P = 3 × VP × IP × PF

10,000 = 3 × 230.94 × IP × PF

IP × PF = 10,000 / (3 × 230.94) ≈ 14.43

We can't determine the exact power factor without knowing the current. The product of current and power factor is 14.43.

Problem 5: Wattmeter Error Correction

A wattmeter has a guaranteed accuracy of ±2% of full scale. The full scale reading is 1000 W, and the meter currently reads 400 W. What is the possible range of actual power consumption?

Solution: The maximum error is 2% of the full scale:

Error = 2% of 1000 W = 0.02 × 1000 W = 20 W

The actual power could be:

Minimum: 400 W - 20 W = 380 W

Maximum: 400 W + 20 W = 420 W

Therefore, the possible range of actual power consumption is 380 W to 420 W.
