How To Desolder Copper Pipe and Resolder Copper Fittings

Warning: Working with soldering equipment involves high temperatures and potentially harmful fumes. Always wear appropriate safety gear, work in a well-ventilated area, and follow proper safety procedures.

Tools and Materials Needed

  • Propane torch
  • Solder
  • Flux
  • Flux brush
  • Emery cloth or sandpaper
  • Pipe cutter
  • Heat-resistant gloves
  • Safety glasses
  • Wet rag

Desoldering Process

1. Turn off the water supply and drain the pipes.

2. Put on your safety glasses and heat-resistant gloves.

3. Light your propane torch and adjust it to a blue flame.

4. Apply heat to the joint you want to desolder, moving the flame around to heat it evenly.

5. Once the solder begins to melt (usually after 30-60 seconds), use pliers to gently pull the fitting off the pipe.

6. If the fitting doesn't come off easily, apply more heat and try again.

7. Use a wet rag to wipe off any excess solder while it's still hot.

Preparing for Resoldering

1. Allow the pipe and fitting to cool completely.

2. Clean both the pipe and the inside of the fitting using emery cloth or sandpaper until they shine.

3. Apply flux to both the outside of the pipe and the inside of the fitting using a flux brush.

Resoldering Process

1. Slide the fitting onto the pipe, ensuring it's fully seated.

2. Light your propane torch and adjust it to a blue flame.

3. Apply heat to the joint, moving the flame around to heat it evenly.

4. Once the flux begins to bubble and smoke (usually after 15-30 seconds), touch the solder to the joint opposite the flame.

5. If the joint is hot enough, the solder will melt and be drawn into the joint. If not, remove the solder and heat for a few more seconds.

6. Once the solder flows freely around the joint, remove the heat and allow the joint to cool naturally.

7. Wipe off any excess solder with a wet rag while it's still hot.

Final Steps

1. Allow the joint to cool completely before testing.

2. Turn the water supply back on and check for leaks.

3. If there are no leaks, your resoldering job is complete.

Remember: If you're unsure about any part of this process or if you're working on pipes that are part of a gas line, it's best to consult a professional plumber.
