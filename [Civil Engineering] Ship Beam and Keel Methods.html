Ship Beam and Keel Methods

1. Line Keel

Definition: The Line Keel is the lowest longitudinal structural member of a ship, extending from bow to stern, upon which the frame of the vessel is erected.

Method:

  1. Design the keel as a continuous structure running the entire length of the ship.
  2. Ensure the keel is strong enough to withstand the ship's weight and resist longitudinal bending.
  3. Integrate the keel with the ship's framing system, connecting it to the hull plates and internal structures.
  4. Use high-strength materials, typically steel, to construct the keel.
  5. Incorporate flood control measures and access points for maintenance when necessary.

2. Latak Beam

Definition: The Latak Beam, also known as the Lateral Beam, is a structural member that runs across the width of a ship, providing lateral strength and support.

Method:

  1. Design the latak beam to span the full width of the ship, from port to starboard.
  2. Calculate the required strength based on the ship's size and expected loads.
  3. Position latak beams at regular intervals along the length of the ship.
  4. Integrate the beams with the ship's framing system and deck structures.
  5. Ensure proper connection to the hull sides for optimal load distribution.

3. T Beam

Definition: A T Beam is a structural member with a T-shaped cross-section, used to provide strength and stiffness while minimizing weight.

Method:

  1. Design the T Beam with a vertical web and a horizontal flange.
  2. Calculate the dimensions of the web and flange based on load requirements.
  3. Fabricate the T Beam using welding or other joining methods.
  4. Install T Beams in areas requiring additional strength, such as deck supports or bulkheads.
  5. Ensure proper alignment and connection to adjacent structural members.

4. Depth of Beam

Definition: The Depth of Beam refers to the vertical dimension of a beam, measured from the top of the upper flange to the bottom of the lower flange.

Method for determining Depth of Beam:

  1. Analyze the loads and span of the beam to determine required strength.
  2. Calculate the minimum depth required to resist bending moments and shear forces.
  3. Consider space constraints and weight limitations in the ship's design.
  4. Use standard beam depth tables or structural engineering software for calculations.
  5. Adjust depth as necessary to meet both structural and design requirements.

5. Size of Beam

Definition: The Size of Beam refers to the overall dimensions of a beam, including its depth, width, and length.

Method for determining Size of Beam:

  1. Determine the required load-bearing capacity of the beam.
  2. Calculate the necessary cross-sectional area to support the load.
  3. Consider the beam's length based on its placement within the ship's structure.
  4. Select appropriate width and depth dimensions to achieve the required cross-sectional area.
  5. Optimize the beam size for weight efficiency while maintaining structural integrity.
  6. Ensure compatibility with connecting structural members and overall ship design.
