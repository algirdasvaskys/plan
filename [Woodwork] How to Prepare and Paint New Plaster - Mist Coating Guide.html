How to Prepare and Paint New Plaster - Mist Coating Guide

Introduction

Painting new plaster requires patience and the right technique to achieve a professional finish. This guide will walk you through the process of preparing and painting new plaster, including the crucial mist coating step.

Materials Needed

  • Paint brushes and rollers
  • Paint tray
  • Sandpaper (fine grit)
  • Dust sheets
  • Masking tape
  • Water-based emulsion paint
  • Clean water
  • Bucket
  • Stirring stick

Step-by-Step Guide

Step 1: Allow the Plaster to Dry

Before painting, ensure the new plaster is completely dry. This typically takes 4-6 weeks, depending on the thickness and environmental conditions.

Warning

Painting on damp plaster can lead to peeling, blistering, and other issues. Be patient and wait for the plaster to dry thoroughly.

Step 2: Prepare the Surface

Once dry, lightly sand the plaster surface with fine-grit sandpaper to remove any bumps or imperfections. Wipe away dust with a dry cloth.

Step 3: Protect Surrounding Areas

Cover floors and furniture with dust sheets. Use masking tape to protect areas you don't want to paint, such as skirting boards and window frames.

Step 4: Prepare the Mist Coat

Create a mist coat by diluting water-based emulsion paint with water. The ratio should be approximately 70% paint to 30% water. Mix thoroughly.

Tip

The mist coat helps the subsequent layers of paint adhere better to the plaster and reduces the risk of peeling.

Step 5: Apply the Mist Coat

Using a roller or brush, apply the mist coat evenly across the plaster surface. Work in sections, maintaining a wet edge to avoid visible lines.

Step 6: Allow to Dry

Let the mist coat dry completely. This usually takes 24 hours, but can vary depending on temperature and humidity.

Step 7: Apply Top Coats

Once the mist coat is dry, apply your chosen paint color. You may need 2-3 coats for full coverage. Allow each coat to dry before applying the next.

Tip

For best results, use a paint specifically designed for new plaster. These often have better adhesion properties.

Conclusion
