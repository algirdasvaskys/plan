Why Resistors Add in Series

When resistors are connected in series, their individual resistances add up to give the total resistance of the circuit. This principle is fundamental in electrical engineering and circuit analysis. Let's explore why this happens.

1. Basic Principle

In a series circuit, the same current flows through each component. The voltage, however, is divided among the components.

2. Ohm's Law

To understand why resistors add in series, we need to recall Ohm's Law:

V = I * R

Where V is voltage, I is current, and R is resistance.

3. Voltage Division

In a series circuit with multiple resistors, the total voltage across the circuit is the sum of the voltages across each resistor:

Vtotal = V1 + V2 + V3 + ...

4. Applying Ohm's Law

We can apply Ohm's Law to each resistor:

V1 = I * R1
V2 = I * R2
V3 = I * R3
...

5. Total Voltage

Substituting these into our voltage division equation:

Vtotal = I * R1 + I * R2 + I * R3 + ...

6. Factoring Out Current

Since the current (I) is the same throughout the series circuit, we can factor it out:

Vtotal = I * (R1 + R2 + R3 + ...)

7. Total Resistance

The expression in parentheses represents the total resistance:

Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...

Conclusion

Therefore, we can conclude that when resistors are connected in series, their individual resistances add up to give the total resistance of the circuit. This is a direct result of how voltage divides across series components and the application of Ohm's Law.
