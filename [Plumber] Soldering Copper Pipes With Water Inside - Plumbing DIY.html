Soldering Copper Pipes With Water Inside - Plumbing DIY

Warning: This is an advanced technique and should only be attempted by experienced DIYers or professionals. Improper execution can lead to injury or damage to your plumbing system. Always prioritize safety and consider consulting a professional plumber.

Introduction

Soldering copper pipes with water inside is a technique used when it's impossible or impractical to completely drain the system. This method uses the water inside the pipe as a heat sink to prevent damage to other parts of the plumbing system.

Materials Needed

  • Propane torch
  • Lead-free solder
  • Flux and flux brush
  • Emery cloth or sandpaper
  • Wet rags
  • Copper pipe and fittings
  • Bread or cotton balls (optional)

Step-by-Step Guide

1. Prepare the work area:

  • Ensure proper ventilation
  • Keep a fire extinguisher nearby
  • Wear safety glasses and heat-resistant gloves

2. Clean the pipe and fitting:

  • Use emery cloth or sandpaper to clean the outside of the pipe and the inside of the fitting
  • Remove any oxidation or debris

3. Apply flux:

  • Use a flux brush to apply a thin, even layer of flux to both the outside of the pipe and the inside of the fitting

4. Prepare the pipe (optional):

  • If possible, stuff a small piece of bread or a cotton ball into the pipe to create a temporary dam
  • This helps prevent water from flowing into the joint area

5. Position wet rags:

  • Place wet rags on either side of the joint to be soldered
  • This helps protect surrounding areas from heat damage

6. Heat the joint:

  • Use the propane torch to heat the fitting, not the pipe
  • Move the flame around the fitting to ensure even heating
  • Continue heating until the flux begins to bubble and sizzle

7. Apply solder:

  • Touch the lead-free solder to the joint where the pipe enters the fitting
  • If the joint is hot enough, the solder will melt and be drawn into the joint
  • Continue applying solder until it appears at the other end of the fitting

8. Cool and clean:

  • Allow the joint to cool naturally
  • Once cool, wipe away any excess flux with a damp cloth

9. Test the joint:

  • Once the system is back in operation, carefully inspect the joint for any leaks

Tips and Precautions

  • Work quickly to prevent the water inside from boiling and creating steam pressure
  • Be prepared for some water to leak out during the process
  • If the pipe is full of water, the solder may not adhere properly. Try to minimize water in the immediate joint area
  • If you're uncomfortable with this technique, it's best to shut off the water supply, drain the system, and solder normally
  • Always follow local plumbing codes and regulations

Remember, while this technique can be useful in emergencies or specific situations, it's generally better to properly drain the system before soldering whenever possible. Safety should always be your top priority when working with plumbing and heat sources.
