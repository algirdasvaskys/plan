Surge Protection Devices (SPDs): Working Principle

Introduction

Surge Protection Devices (SPDs), also known as Surge Arresters or Overvoltage Protection Devices, are crucial components in electrical systems. They protect sensitive equipment from voltage spikes and transients that can damage or destroy electronic devices.

Basic Working Principle

The primary function of an SPD is to limit transient overvoltages and divert surge currents. It achieves this through the following mechanisms:

  1. Voltage-dependent resistance: SPDs contain components (such as Metal Oxide Varistors or MOVs) that have high resistance at normal voltage levels but low resistance during overvoltage events.
  2. Current diversion: When a surge occurs, the SPD provides a low-impedance path to ground, diverting the excess current away from the protected equipment.
  3. Voltage clamping: SPDs limit the voltage to a safe level, typically slightly above the normal operating voltage of the protected equipment.

Types of SPDs

There are several types of SPDs, each with its own characteristics:

  • Voltage Switching Type: These devices (like spark gaps) create a short circuit when the voltage exceeds a threshold.
  • Voltage Limiting Type: These devices (like MOVs) gradually increase their conductivity as voltage increases.
  • Combination Type: These devices combine both switching and limiting technologies for enhanced protection.

SPD Operation Stages

The operation of an SPD can be divided into three main stages:

  1. Normal operation: The SPD remains in a high-impedance state, allowing normal current flow.
  2. Surge detection: When a voltage surge is detected, the SPD quickly switches to a low-impedance state.
  3. Surge suppression: The SPD diverts the surge current to ground and clamps the voltage to a safe level.
