Kelvin Double Bridge: Low Resistance Measurement Method

Introduction

The Kelvin Double Bridge is a precision method used for measuring very low resistances, typically in the range of 1 Ω or less. It's an improvement over the Wheatstone bridge, which becomes inaccurate for low resistance measurements due to the effects of contact and lead resistances.

Principle

The Kelvin Double Bridge uses a double set of ratio arms to eliminate the effects of lead and contact resistances. It consists of two bridge circuits: the main bridge and an auxiliary bridge.

The circuit consists of:

  • Unknown resistance (Rx)
  • Standard resistance (Rs)
  • Two pairs of ratio arms (R1, R2 and r1, r2)
  • Galvanometer (G)
  • Battery (B)

Working Principle

When the bridge is balanced (no current through the galvanometer), the following equation holds:

Rx / Rs = R1 / R2 = r1 / r2

Advantages

  • High accuracy for very low resistance measurements
  • Eliminates errors due to lead and contact resistances
  • Suitable for precision measurements in laboratories

Limitations

  • Complex setup compared to simpler bridge circuits
  • Requires a high-quality standard resistor
  • Not suitable for high resistance measurements

Problem Solving

Problem 1

In a Kelvin Double Bridge, R1 = 100 Ω, R2 = 10 Ω, r1 = 10 Ω, r2 = 1 Ω, and Rs = 0.1 Ω. Calculate the unknown resistance Rx.

Solution

Using the balance equation: Rx / Rs = R1 / R2 = r1 / r2

Rx / 0.1 = 100 / 10 = 10 / 1 = 10

Rx = 0.1 × 10 = 1 Ω

Therefore, the unknown resistance Rx is 1 Ω.

Problem 2

A Kelvin Double Bridge is used to measure a low resistance. The standard resistor Rs is 0.01 Ω. The ratio arms are set such that R1 = 1000 Ω, R2 = 100 Ω, r1 = 100 Ω, and r2 = 10 Ω. What is the value of the unknown resistance Rx?

Solution

Using the balance equation: Rx / Rs = R1 / R2 = r1 / r2

Rx / 0.01 = 1000 / 100 = 100 / 10 = 10

Rx = 0.01 × 10 = 0.1 Ω

Therefore, the unknown resistance Rx is 0.1 Ω.

Conclusion

The Kelvin Double Bridge is a powerful tool for measuring very low resistances with high accuracy. By understanding its principles and applying them correctly, engineers and scientists can make precise measurements in various applications, from electrical component testing to material characterization.
