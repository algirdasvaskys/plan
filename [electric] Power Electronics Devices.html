Power Electronics Devices

1. Cycloconverter

A cycloconverter is a power electronic device that converts AC power at one frequency to AC power at another frequency, typically lower, without an intermediate DC link.

Method:

  • Uses naturally commutated thyristors or SCRs
  • Directly converts AC to AC
  • Controls the firing angle of thyristors to achieve desired output frequency
  • Typically used for large motor drives and induction heating

2. Chopper

A chopper is a static power electronic device that converts fixed DC input to a variable DC output voltage.

Method:

  • Uses power semiconductor switches (e.g., MOSFETs, IGBTs)
  • Rapidly switches the input DC on and off
  • Controls the duty cycle to adjust the average output voltage
  • Commonly used in DC motor speed control and DC-DC converters

3. Controlled Rectifier

A controlled rectifier converts AC input to a controllable DC output using thyristors or other controllable switches.

Method:

  • Uses thyristors (SCRs) as switching devices
  • Controls the firing angle of thyristors to adjust the output voltage
  • Can be single-phase or three-phase
  • Commonly used in industrial power supplies and motor drives

4. Uncontrolled Rectifier

An uncontrolled rectifier converts AC input to a fixed DC output using diodes.

Method:

  • Uses diodes as rectifying elements
  • No control over the output voltage (except for input voltage changes)
  • Can be half-wave or full-wave, single-phase or three-phase
  • Commonly used in simple power supplies and battery chargers

5. AC Voltage Controller

An AC voltage controller is a power electronic device that varies the RMS value of the AC output voltage without changing its frequency.

Method:

  • Uses thyristors or triacs as switching devices
  • Controls the firing angle to adjust the portion of each half-cycle that is conducted
  • Can be single-phase or three-phase
  • Commonly used in lighting control, heating applications, and soft starters for AC motors
