Basic Refrigeration Cycle - How It Works

The basic refrigeration cycle is a process used in various cooling systems, including air conditioners and refrigerators. It works by transferring heat from inside a space (like a refrigerator or a room) to the outside environment. This process involves four main components and four stages.

Main Components

  • Compressor
  • Condenser
  • Expansion Valve
  • Evaporator

Refrigeration Cycle Diagram

The Four Stages of the Refrigeration Cycle

1. Compression

The cycle begins with the compressor. It takes in low-pressure, low-temperature refrigerant gas and compresses it. This process increases both the pressure and temperature of the refrigerant. The refrigerant leaves the compressor as a high-pressure, high-temperature gas.

2. Condensation

The hot, high-pressure refrigerant gas then enters the condenser. Here, it releases its heat to the surrounding environment (usually outside air). As it cools, the refrigerant condenses from a gas into a liquid, while still maintaining high pressure.

3. Expansion

The high-pressure liquid refrigerant then passes through an expansion valve. This valve allows the refrigerant to expand quickly, which causes a sudden drop in pressure. This pressure drop results in a significant temperature decrease, turning some of the liquid refrigerant into a cold, low-pressure mixture of liquid and vapor.

4. Evaporation

Finally, the cold refrigerant enters the evaporator. Here, it absorbs heat from the surrounding area (inside the refrigerator or the room to be cooled). This heat causes the remaining liquid refrigerant to evaporate into a gas. The refrigerant leaves the evaporator as a low-pressure, low-temperature gas, ready to enter the compressor again and repeat the cycle.

Conclusion

This continuous cycle effectively moves heat from inside the cooled space to the outside environment. The efficiency of this process depends on various factors, including the type of refrigerant used, the design of the components, and the temperature difference between the inside and outside environments.
