Synchronous Motors: Principles and Operation

Introduction

A synchronous motor is an AC motor that rotates at a speed directly proportional to the frequency of the supplied current. Unlike induction motors, the rotor of a synchronous motor rotates at the same speed as the rotating magnetic field in the stator.

Basic Principles

1. Stator Construction

The stator of a synchronous motor is similar to that of an induction motor. It consists of a cylindrical iron frame with slots on its inner periphery. These slots house a three-phase winding, which, when energized with three-phase AC, produces a rotating magnetic field.

2. Rotor Construction

The rotor of a synchronous motor can be of two types:

  • Salient pole type: Used for low-speed motors, with projecting poles and a DC field winding.
  • Cylindrical type: Used for high-speed motors, with a cylindrical core and slots for the field winding.

The rotor is excited by a separate DC source, creating a strong, constant magnetic field.

3. Synchronous Speed

The speed of rotation of the magnetic field (synchronous speed) is given by the formula:

Ns = 120f / P

Where:

  • Ns is the synchronous speed in RPM
  • f is the frequency of the AC supply in Hz
  • P is the number of poles

4. Operating Principle

1. The stator windings are energized, creating a rotating magnetic field.

2. The rotor is excited by DC, creating a strong magnetic field.

3. The rotor field locks with the stator field, causing the rotor to rotate at the same speed as the stator field.

4. Once synchronized, the rotor maintains this speed under varying loads.

5. Starting Methods

Synchronous motors cannot self-start. Common starting methods include:

  • Using a small induction motor (pony motor)
  • Damper windings for asynchronous starting
  • Variable frequency drive (VFD) for controlled acceleration

Advantages and Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Constant speed operation regardless of load
  • High efficiency, especially at full load
  • Power factor correction capability
  • Precise speed control in applications requiring synchronization

Disadvantages

  • Cannot self-start, requiring additional starting mechanisms
  • More complex and expensive than induction motors
  • Requires a DC excitation source for the rotor
  • Can "fall out of step" if overloaded, causing stoppage

Applications

Synchronous motors are used in various industrial applications, including:

  • Power factor correction in industrial plants
  • Constant speed drives in paper mills, textile mills, and rolling mills
  • Compressors and pumps in large industrial processes
  • Conveyor systems requiring precise speed control
  • Generator drives in power plants

Conclusion

Synchronous motors offer unique advantages in terms of speed control, efficiency, and power factor correction. Understanding their principles of operation is crucial for engineers and technicians working with industrial motor systems. While they have some limitations, their specific characteristics make them invaluable in many high-power and precision applications.
