AC Contactor Types and Motor Starter Contactors

Differences Between AC1, AC2, and AC3 Contactors

Type Description Application
AC1 Non-inductive or slightly inductive loads Resistive heating, lighting, distribution
AC2 Slip-ring motors: starting, plugging Cranes, lifts, conveyors
AC3 Squirrel-cage motors: starting, switching off during running Pumps, fans, compressors

Detailed Explanation

AC1 Contactors

AC1 contactors are designed for non-inductive or slightly inductive loads. They are typically used in applications where the load does not produce significant inrush currents or where the breaking current is similar to the normal operating current.

AC2 Contactors

AC2 contactors are specifically designed for slip-ring motors. These motors have a higher starting current and require special consideration during starting and plugging (rapid reversal) operations. AC2 contactors can handle these high currents and frequent switching operations.

AC3 Contactors

AC3 contactors are the most common type used for motor starting applications, particularly for squirrel-cage motors. They are designed to handle high inrush currents during motor starting (which can be 5-7 times the rated current) and can safely interrupt the current when the motor is running at full speed.

Types of Contactors Used for Motor Starters

Motor starters typically use AC3-rated contactors due to their ability to handle the high inrush currents associated with motor starting. However, the specific type of contactor used may vary based on the motor size, application, and starting method. Here are some common types:

  1. Direct-on-line (DOL) starters: Use AC3 contactors for simple on/off control of smaller motors.
  2. Star-Delta starters: Use multiple AC3 contactors to reduce starting current for larger motors.
  3. Soft starters: Use specialized solid-state contactors to gradually increase voltage during starting.
  4. Variable Frequency Drives (VFDs): Use solid-state power electronics for advanced motor control.

Important Note:

When selecting a contactor for a motor starter application, always consider the following factors:

  • Motor full-load current
  • Starting current and duration
  • Duty cycle and switching frequency
  • Environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, etc.)
  • Control voltage requirements
  • Any specific industry or application standards

Always consult the manufacturer's specifications and local electrical codes when selecting and installing motor starters and contactors.
