SCR: Construction, Working & Characteristics

1. SCR Construction

A Silicon Controlled Rectifier (SCR) is a four-layer semiconductor device with three terminals: Anode, Cathode, and Gate. Its construction consists of alternating P-type and N-type silicon layers:

  • P-type layer (Anode)
  • N-type layer
  • P-type layer
  • N-type layer (Cathode)

2. Working Principle

The SCR operates based on the following principles:

  1. Off State: In the absence of gate current, the SCR blocks current flow in both directions.
  2. Turn-On: When a positive voltage is applied to the gate relative to the cathode, and the anode is positive with respect to the cathode, the SCR turns on and conducts current.
  3. Latching: Once turned on, the SCR continues to conduct even if the gate signal is removed, as long as the anode current remains above the holding current.
  4. Turn-Off: The SCR turns off when the anode current falls below the holding current or when the anode-cathode voltage is reversed.

3. Characteristics

The SCR exhibits the following key characteristics:

  • Forward Blocking: High resistance when forward-biased but not triggered.
  • Forward Conduction: Low resistance when triggered and forward-biased.
  • Reverse Blocking: High resistance when reverse-biased.
  • Gate Trigger Current: Minimum current required to turn on the SCR.
  • Holding Current: Minimum anode current required to keep the SCR in conduction state.
  • Latching Current: Minimum anode current required to maintain conduction immediately after triggering and gate signal removal.

4. Applications in Power Electronics

SCRs are widely used in power electronics applications, including:

  • AC to DC power conversion
  • Motor speed control
  • Light dimmers
  • Induction heating
  • Voltage regulation
  • Static VAR compensation
  • Circuit breakers
