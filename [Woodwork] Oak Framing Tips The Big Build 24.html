Oak Framing Tips: The Big Build 24

1. Preparing Your Oak

Before you begin framing, ensure your oak is properly prepared:

  • Use air-dried oak with a moisture content of 20-30%
  • Allow for shrinkage in your designs, especially for wider timbers
  • Check for any twists or bows in the timber and plan accordingly

2. Fitting Staddle Stones

Staddle stones are traditional supports used to elevate timber frames off the ground. Here's how to fit them:

  1. Prepare a level foundation for each staddle stone
  2. Place the staddle stones at the corners and key points of your frame
  3. Use a laser level to ensure all stones are at the same height
  4. Secure the oak posts to the staddle stones using metal brackets or traditional joinery methods
Tip: Always check local building regulations regarding the use of staddle stones, as modern building codes may require additional foundation work.

3. Drilling for Pegs

Wooden pegs are crucial in traditional oak framing. Follow these steps for drilling:

  1. Mark your drill points carefully, ensuring they align with your joinery plan
  2. Use a drill bit slightly smaller than your peg diameter (usually 1/16" smaller)
  3. Drill slowly and steadily to avoid splintering the exit point
  4. Clean out any debris from the holes before inserting pegs
Warning: Always wear safety goggles and use sharp, high-quality drill bits to prevent injury and ensure clean holes.

4. Choosing and Preparing Pegs

The right pegs are essential for strong joints:

  • Use hardwood pegs, preferably oak or ash
  • Ensure pegs are straight-grained and free from knots
  • Taper the ends of the pegs slightly to ease insertion
  • Consider using octagonal pegs for a traditional look and better grip

5. Assembling the Frame

When putting your frame together:

  • Dry-fit all joints before final assembly
  • Use draw-boring techniques for tighter joints
  • Apply a small amount of glue in peg holes for added strength
  • Drive pegs in firmly, but avoid over-hammering

6. Finishing Touches

To complete your oak frame:

  • Trim excess peg length, leaving about 1/4" proud of the surface
  • Sand any rough areas, but maintain the natural character of the oak
  • Apply a suitable finish to protect the wood, if desired
Final Tip: Remember that oak framing is a skilled craft. If you're new to this, consider taking a course or working alongside an experienced framer for your first project.
