DOL Starter Contactor Holding Circuit without Using NO Contact

Introduction

A Direct On Line (DOL) starter is a common method used to start a three-phase induction motor. Typically, the contactor holding circuit in a DOL starter uses a normally open (NO) auxiliary contact. However, it's possible to create a holding circuit without using this NO contact. This document explains the method and its advantages.

Method Explanation

  1. Power Supply: The control circuit is powered from one of the three phases (L1 in this case).
  2. Stop Button: A normally closed (NC) stop button is connected in series with the control circuit.
  3. Start Button: A normally open (NO) start button is connected in parallel with the contactor coil.
  4. Contactor Coil: The contactor coil is connected directly to the control circuit without using its own auxiliary NO contact.
  5. Holding Circuit: The holding circuit is created by the inherent latching of the contactor when energized.

Operation

  1. When the start button is pressed, current flows through the contactor coil, energizing it.
  2. The main contacts of the contactor close, connecting the motor to the three-phase supply.
  3. The contactor remains energized even after releasing the start button due to the continuous current flow through the closed main contacts.
  4. To stop the motor, the stop button is pressed, breaking the control circuit and de-energizing the contactor.

Advantages

  • Simplified circuit design with fewer components
  • Reduced wiring complexity
  • Lower cost due to elimination of auxiliary NO contact
  • Increased reliability due to fewer potential points of failure

Considerations

While this method is effective, it's important to note that it may not be suitable for all applications. In some cases, the traditional method using an NO auxiliary contact might be preferred, especially in situations where additional control or safety features are required.

Conclusion

The DOL starter contactor holding circuit without using an NO contact offers a simplified and cost-effective solution for motor starting applications. By understanding this method, engineers and technicians can choose the most appropriate circuit design for their specific needs.
